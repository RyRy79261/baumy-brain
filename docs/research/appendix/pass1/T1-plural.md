# T1-plural — Memory is plural (the systems partition: working/episodic/semantic/procedural)

**Topic verdict:** mixed

## Adjudicated claims

### A) The working/long-term and declarative/procedural/episodic/semantic partition is SHARED across cognitive science (Tulving, Baddeley, Squire, Atkinson-Shiffrin) and AI cognitive architectures (CoALA <- Soar, ACT-R).
- **Verdict:** weakened-but-stands (confidence: high)
- **Strongest support:** The literal sharing is uncontested and documented: CoALA (Sumers et al., TMLR 2024, arXiv:2309.02427) explicitly partitions agent memory into working + episodic/semantic/procedural and states it is mirroring cognitive psychology; Soar and ACT-R (Anderson et al. 2004) really do carry declarative/procedural + episodic/semantic stores. The coarse cuts are additionally lesion-grounded (H.M.: declarative destroyed / procedural intact; K.C.: episodic abolished / semantic preserved), so 'memory is plural at the level of coarse functional dissociations' holds under every camp, even the critics.
- **Strongest attack:** The steelman's load-bearing escalation — 'three INDEPENDENT evidentiary bases / triply-derived CONVERGENT partition' — fails a genealogical test. The AI branch is downstream of the psychology, not parallel to it: ACT-R's declarative/procedural traces to Ryle (1949) via Anderson's ACT program; Newell & Simon's Human Problem Solving (1972) is itself a cognitive-psychology monograph using the era's 'short-term memory'; and CoALA is (by the steelman's own Claim-B concession) an admitted deliberate import. Atkinson-Shiffrin's store/buffer scheme was itself the computer metaphor. So 'convergence' is largely shared ancestry + borrowing (autocorrelation), and the double dissociations (per Dunn & Kirsner 1988) do not force separate modules nor validate the specific four-box scheme.
- **Open question:** How much genuinely independent warrant survives once the borrowed labels are stripped — i.e., would engineering pressure (bounded context vs external store; retrievable knowledge vs compiled policy/weights) have produced the fast/slow and declarative/procedural cuts even with zero contact with Tulving/Squire? That residue, not the label-sharing, is the only thing that could rescue any 'convergence' reading.

### B) Labeling an agent store 'episodic' is a productive functional ANALOGY, not evidence agents 'have' episodic memory in Tulving's sense.
- **Verdict:** weakened-but-stands (confidence: high)
- **Strongest support:** The possession-denial is well-grounded and even corroborated by the target literature itself: the LLM-'episodic-memory' preprints frame the capability as missing / to-be-engineered / benchmarked-as-deficient, and CoALA never claims autonoetic re-experiencing. The false-equivalence diagnosis is clean and durable: engineers imported the storage/retrieval structure and silently dropped the consciousness axis (procedural↔anoetic, semantic↔noetic, episodic↔autonoetic) that individuates the human kinds. A retrieved timestamped trace is functionally closer to semantic memory of an event than to episodic re-experiencing.
- **Strongest attack:** The sharpness ('category error,' 'precisely NOT episodic memory') is bought by silently selecting the strongest, most exclusionary definition. The autonoetic criterion is Tulving's stipulation, inferred only from first-person Remember/Know report — and Dunn (2004) argues R/K reduces to a single signal-detection strength dimension, so the criterion is contested even for humans. Comparative cognition deliberately abandoned it: Clayton & Dickinson (1998, Nature) coined 'episodic-like memory' with an operational what-where-when test precisely because behavioral tasks cannot address autonoetic consciousness. Under that behavioral criterion — the one the field uses for every non-verbal system — the human↔machine line is fuzzy, not a category error. The verdict is definition-relative, not a fact.
- **Open question:** Under the operational what-where-when criterion (not autonoetic), do current agent memory stores actually satisfy episodic-LIKE memory — including the integrated, flexible, one-trial-bound representation animal work requires — or only a trivial 'queryable timestamped log' version that even the behavioral criterion would reject? The fuzziness the red team invokes may itself dissolve at the stricter operational reading.

### C) Whether these are separate systems vs a continuum (SPI / embedded-processes), and whether a distinct short-term store exists vs 'activated LTM', is itself contested in psychology.
- **Verdict:** survives (confidence: high)
- **Strongest support:** Straightforwardly true and top-journal live: the Norris (2017) -> Cowan et al. (2019) exchange in Psychological Bulletin is a direct, unresolved dispute over whether STM is a separate store or activated LTM; Cowan's embedded-processes model denies an architecturally separate STM; Cabeza & Moscovitch (2013) frame systems / processing-modes / components as an unresolved three-way tension (with neuroimaging cited AGAINST the clean 'systems' cut); Tulving's own SPI model makes episodic encoding dependent on semantic; Sherry & Schacter (1987) show 'what counts as a system' is a theoretical stipulation. Every citation checks out.
- **Strongest attack:** There is no real attack on C's truth — the red team concedes it. Its only move is that C 'proves too much': taken seriously it retroactively convicts the topic title and Claim A of over-precision, since it shows the separate STM store is disputed, the episodic/semantic border is graded, and a serious slice of the field says 'separate stores' may be the wrong ontology (one substrate in different activation states). That is a consequence, not a rebuttal — and the steelman already conceded it ('flag it as contested at the seams is MORE rigorous').
- **Open question:** If working memory is activated LTM and recollection is an operation-at-retrieval rather than content in a dedicated subsystem, does a store-based ontology (n distinct memory stores) misdescribe the human architecture badly enough that ANY agent taxonomy built on discrete stores is analogizing to a structure a substantial camp says does not exist as discrete stores — regardless of the labels used?

**Strawman attacks flagged:** The red team charges that the steelman 'slides... to underwriting the four-store scheme' via the double dissociations. The steelman was actually more careful: it explicitly scoped H.M. to declarative/procedural and K.C. to episodic/semantic, and rested only 'memory is plural' on the dissociations — it did NOT claim the dissociations prove the four-box partition. **Overstated enrichment flagged:** The single biggest overstatement: the original Claim A said only that the partition is 'SHARED' across the two fields; the steelman escalated this to 'triply-derived CONVERGENT partition,' 'three INDEPENDENT evidentiary bases,' 'arrived at independently.' ## CLAIM A — The partition is genuinely shared across cognitive science and AI cognitive-architecture work

**Strongest defensible formulation:** *A small set of memory distinctions — a fast, capacity-limited working store vs. slow durable long-term stores, and within long-term memory a split between retrievable declarative content (facts/events) and compiled procedural know-how — was arrived at independently by experimental psychology, systems neuroscience, and symbolic AI, and CoALA imports exactly that set to organize LLM agents.* This is not one lab's taxonomy generalized; it is a convergent partition with three independent evidentiary bases.

**The cognitive-science lineage (all verified):**

- **Atkinson & Shiffrin (1968), "Human Memory: A Proposed System and Its Control Processes,"** in Spence & Spence (eds.), *The Psychology of Learning and Motivation*, vol. 2 — the "modal model": sensory register → short-term store → long-term store, with *control processes* operating over them. This is the direct structural ancestor of the working/long-term split. (Its 50-year legacy is itself reviewed: Malmberg, Raaijmakers & Shiffrin, *Memory & Cognition* 47, 2019.)
- **Tulving (1972), "Episodic and Semantic Memory,"** in Tulving & Donaldson (eds.), *Organization of Memory*, pp. 381–403, Academic Press — the episodic/semantic dissociation within declarative long-term memory.
- **Baddeley & Hitch (1974), "Working Memory,"** in Bower (ed.), *The Psychology of Learning and Motivation*, vol. 8 — replaces the passive STS with a multi-component *working* memory (central executive, phonological loop, visuospatial sketchpad); **Baddeley (2000), "The episodic buffer: a new component of working memory?"** *Trends in Cognitive Sciences* 4(11):417–423 (PubMed 11058819) adds the binding buffer. This is the direct ancestor of "working memory" as a manipulable scratchpad rather than a storage bin.
- **Squire's declarative/nondeclarative taxonomy** — **Squire (1992),** *Psychological Review* 99(2):195–231; **Squire & Zola (1996), "Structure and function of declarative and nondeclarative memory systems,"** *PNAS* 93(24):13515–13522; **Squire (2004), "Memory systems of the brain: a brief history and current perspective,"** *Neurobiology of Learning and Memory* 82(3):171–177. Nondeclarative subsumes **procedural** (skills/habits), priming, conditioning, non-associative learning. This is the branch that grounds "procedural memory" neuroanatomically (basal ganglia/cerebellum) as distinct from declarative (medial temporal lobe/diencephalon).

**The AI lineage CoALA inherits (verified):**

- **CoALA — Sumers, Yao, Narasimhan & Griffiths, "Cognitive Architectures for Language Agents,"** *Transactions on Machine Learning Research* (TMLR), 2024; arXiv:2309.02427 (OpenReview `1i6ZCvflQJ`). It explicitly partitions agent memory into **working memory + three long-term stores (episodic, semantic, procedural)** and states it is *mirroring* cognitive-psychology distinctions.
- **The symbolic-AI ancestors are real, not decorative.** **Soar** (Laird, *The Soar Cognitive Architecture*, MIT Press, 2012; and Nuxoll & Laird's episodic-memory work) independently splits long-term memory into **procedural (production rules), semantic, and episodic** stores over a working memory. **ACT-R** (Anderson et al., "An integrated theory of the mind," *Psychological Review* 111(4):1036–1060, 2004) is built on a **declarative/procedural** division with activation-based declarative retrieval and production-compiled procedural knowledge. Newell & Simon's architectures reached the working-vs-long-term and declarative-vs-procedural splits from *engineering* pressure (what a problem-solver must hold active vs. store), not from reading Tulving.

**Where the cross-field convergence is genuinely DEEP (the honest strong case):**

1. **Working vs. long-term is a convergent architectural necessity, not a metaphor.** Every field independently hits the same constraint: a system that reasons over a stream needs a *small, fast, rapidly-overwritten* buffer distinct from a *large, slow, durable* store. Psychology gets it from serial-position/recency dissociations and dual-task interference; neuroscience gets it from prefrontal vs. MTL dissociation; AI gets it from context-window vs. external-store engineering. Three independent derivations converging on the same cut is exactly what "deep" convergence looks like.
2. **Declarative(semantic) vs. procedural maps onto a real computational cleavage that recurs in ML.** "Facts you can retrieve and state" vs. "skills compiled into how the system acts" is the same cut ML draws between *retrievable parametric/nonparametric knowledge* and *policy/weights*. Anderson's production-compilation and Squire's habit-learning describe the same phenomenon — knowledge that starts declarative and is compiled into non-introspectable procedure — that shows up in agents as prompts/tool-specs (declarative) vs. fine-tuned behavior (procedural). This isn't shared vocabulary; it's a shared *functional* distinction with independent support.
3. **The double dissociation is the load-bearing evidence, and it is neurological, not just behavioral.** Patient **H.M.** (Scoville & Milner, "Loss of recent memory after bilateral hippocampal lesions," *J. Neurol. Neurosurg. Psychiatry* 20(1):11–21, 1957): declarative memory destroyed, procedural (mirror-drawing) intact. Patient **K.C.** (Tulving): episodic autobiographical memory abolished, semantic knowledge preserved. These clinical dissociations mean the partition earns its keep as *separable mechanisms*, not just descriptive bins — the strongest possible warrant for treating memory as plural.

**Bottom line for A:** The claim is well-supported. The partition is not a single tradition's artifact; it is triply-derived (behavioral psychology, lesion/imaging neuroscience, symbolic-AI engineering), and CoALA's import of it is a documented, peer-reviewed (TMLR) act of borrowing an already-convergent scheme.

## CLAIM B — The AI re-derivation is a productive ANALOGY, not evidence that agents "have" episodic memory

**Strongest defensible formulation:** *When an agent framework labels a store "episodic," it is naming a functional role (single-shot storage of timestamped, instance-specific experience for later retrieval), not asserting the phenomenological kind Tulving defined. The analogy is productive because the functional role is real and useful; it is not evidence of episodic memory in the psychological sense because the defining criterion of that kind — autonoetic re-experiencing — is absent and, on current architectures, untested/untestable.*

**Why the analogy is genuinely productive (steelman the "useful" half honestly):**

- CoALA's own framing is *analogical by design*: it is a taxonomy/organizing lens for surveying agents, not a claim of psychological realism. Using it, "episodic" cleanly names the store of past interaction traces, "semantic" the store of extracted facts, "procedural" the store of skills/code — and that vocabulary does real classificatory work across otherwise-incommensurable agent designs. That utility is the correct, defensible sense of "productive analogy."
- The functional role labeled "episodic" — **single-shot, instance-specific, context-bound storage** — is a real capability gap in LLMs (bounded context window, no persistence across sessions), which is why a live research literature treats it as a target. *Flagged as preprints, venues NOT asserted:* "Episodic Memory is the Missing Piece for Long-Term LLM Agents" (arXiv:2502.06975); EM-LLM (arXiv:2407.09450); Sequence Order Recall Tasks for assessing LLM episodic memory (arXiv:2410.08133); Episodic Memories Generation & Evaluation Benchmark (arXiv:2501.13121). The honest reading of this literature *supports* claim B: it frames episodic memory as **missing / to-be-engineered / benchmarked-as-deficient**, i.e., a functional capability, not a possessed mental faculty.

**Why it is NOT evidence agents "have" episodic memory (the load-bearing distinction):**

- **Tulving's definition is criterial, and the criterion is autonoetic consciousness.** Tulving (1985, "Memory and consciousness," *Canadian Psychology* 26(1):1–12; 2002, "Episodic memory: from mind to brain," *Annual Review of Psychology* 53:1–25) defines episodic memory by **autonoetic ("self-knowing") awareness** and **mental time travel** — first-person re-experiencing of a specific past event as one's own. This is not incidental packaging; it is the property that distinguishes episodic from semantic memory of the same event. An agent that retrieves a timestamped log entry exhibits the *retrieval function* with none of the *phenomenological criterion*. On Tulving's own terms, "episodic retrieval without autonoetic consciousness" is precisely *not* episodic memory (see the framing in "Episodic memory without autonoetic consciousness," PMC11496718).
- **The three-consciousness mapping makes the point sharp:** procedural↔anoetic, semantic↔noetic, episodic↔autonoetic. The agent literature borrows the *storage/retrieval* structure while silently dropping the *consciousness* axis that individuates the human kinds. Importing the label without the criterion is analogy; asserting possession would be a category error.
- **A vivid-log is a semantic record of an episode, not an episodic memory.** This is exactly the human distinction (K.C. retains semantic facts about his life with no episodic re-experiencing). An agent's retrieved trace is functionally closer to *semantic memory of an event* than to episodic memory — which is why calling it "episodic" is analogy, not identity.

**Bottom line for B:** Strongly defensible. The analogy earns its keep as an engineering/organizing device (and the very research pursuing it treats episodic memory as *absent and to-be-built*), while the defining criterion of the psychological kind (autonoetic mental time travel) is neither present nor claimed. Ascribing possession would over-read a functional label.

## CLAIM C — Systems-vs-continuum, and whether a distinct short-term store exists, is itself contested in psychology

**Strongest defensible formulation:** *The systems partition is a live scientific debate, not settled doctrine — which means a baseline that treats it as clean fact is over-claiming. Three specific, well-documented controversies show the partition's seams.* This claim is the most straightforwardly true of the three, and the honest steelman is to show the contest is real and substantive, not fringe.

**Contest 1 — Separate short-term store vs. "activated LTM."** This is a genuine, current, named dispute in a top journal:
- **Cowan's embedded-processes model** treats working memory as the *activated subset of long-term memory* plus a ~4-item focus of attention — i.e., **no architecturally separate STM store** (Cowan, "The magical number 4," *Behavioral and Brain Sciences* 24(1):87–114, 2001; and the embedded-processes program).
- **Norris (2017), "Short-term memory and long-term memory are still different,"** *Psychological Bulletin* 143(9):992–1009 (PubMed 28530428) argues the opposite from computational demands — STM must store *novel* items, *multiple tokens of one type*, and support *variable binding* (his "1, 3, 1" ordering argument), none achievable by mere LTM activation.
- **Cowan et al. (2019), "Short-term memory based on activated long-term memory: A review in response to Norris (2017),"** *Psychological Bulletin* (PubMed 31328941) is the direct rebuttal. A 2017→2019 exchange in *Psychological Bulletin* is dispositive evidence the question is *open*.

**Contest 2 — Discrete systems vs. a continuum / interacting processes.** The episodic/semantic boundary is itself argued to be graded, and a whole "processing" tradition rejects "systems" framing:
- **Tulving's own SPI model** (Tulving, 1995, in Gazzaniga (ed.), *The Cognitive Neurosciences*) already softens "independent systems" into *serial encoding, parallel storage, independent retrieval* — encoding into episodic memory is **dependent on** prior semantic encoding, i.e., the systems are not cleanly modular at encoding.
- **Systems-vs-processing debate:** the **component-process framework** (Roediger, Buckner & McDermott, "Components of processing," 1999) holds there are not a handful of systems but *dozens of processing components* recruited in combinations. **Cabeza & Moscovitch (2013), "Memory Systems, Processing Modes, and Components: Functional Neuroimaging Evidence,"** *Perspectives on Psychological Science* 8(1):49–55 (PMC3806137) reviews this as an unresolved three-way tension (systems / processing modes / components). Neuroimaging was claimed to favor components — i.e., the "systems" cut is contested *by the neuroscience that was supposed to vindicate it*.
- Recent work pushes past the episodic–semantic dichotomy toward multidimensional/continuum models (e.g., "Beyond the episodic–semantic continuum: the multidimensional model of mental representations," PMC11449204, 2024), and retrieval-based accounts argue recollection is an operation at retrieval rather than content in a dedicated subsystem (Frontiers in Behavioral Neuroscience 7:3, 2013).

**Contest 3 — What even counts as a "system."** The criterial definition (**Sherry & Schacter, "The evolution of multiple memory systems," *Psychological Review* 94(4):439–454, 1987** — a system is a distinct acquisition/retention/retrieval mechanism with its own operating rules) is itself a *theoretical stipulation*. Whether episodic and semantic meet it, or are two settings of one declarative mechanism, is exactly what's disputed.

**Where this strengthens rather than weakens the baseline (the honest steelman turn):** The *coarse* cuts are robust under all camps — even Cowan keeps a working/long-term functional distinction; even the components camp keeps declarative/nondeclarative dissociations (they're lesion-grounded). What's contested is the *fine* structure: strict modularity, a dedicated STM buffer, and hard episodic/semantic borders. So the defensible baseline is: **"memory is plural" is well-supported at the level of coarse functional dissociations; the specific four-box partition is a useful idealization whose module boundaries are actively debated.** A baseline that states the partition *and flags it as contested at the seams* is more rigorous than one that asserts four clean boxes — and claim C is the reason.

## Cross-field convergence: deep vs. shallow, stated honestly

- **DEEP:** working-vs-long-term (three independent derivations hitting the same capacity/durability constraint); declarative-vs-procedural (a real computational cleavage, lesion-doubly-dissociated, mirrored in ML's knowledge-vs-policy split). These would survive translation between fields.
- **SHALLOW / vocabulary-level:** the agent use of "episodic" — it borrows the storage/retrieval role while dropping the autonoetic criterion that *defines* the human kind (this is exactly claim B). Convergence here is analogical, and the literature itself treats agent episodic memory as *missing*, corroborating the modesty.
- **CONTESTED EVEN WITHIN COGNITIVE SCIENCE:** whether these are "systems" at all vs. processing components, and whether a separate STM store exists (claim C) — so the convergence, where real, is a convergence onto *functional dissociations*, not onto a settled modular architecture.

**Net:** All three claims are defensible on the strong evidence. A is triply-grounded and well-cited; B is the correct, modest reading that the agent literature's own framing supports; C is straightforwardly true and, handled well, makes the baseline *more* rigorous rather than undercutting it.

## CLAIM A — "triply-derived convergent partition." The weakest of the three, despite looking strongest.

The steelman's whole force rests on **independence**: "three independent evidentiary bases," "arrived at independently by experimental psychology, systems neuroscience, and symbolic AI," "three independent derivations converging on the same cut is exactly what deep convergence looks like." Break independence and "convergence" collapses into **shared ancestry + deliberate borrowing** — which is not evidence of a real joint, it's evidence of a citation lineage.

**1. The AI "derivation" is not independent — it is downstream of the very psychology it allegedly converges with (the genealogical confound).**
- ACT-R's declarative/procedural split is not engineering-native. Anderson took it from the **knowing-that / knowing-how** distinction of **Ryle, *The Concept of Mind* (1949)**, imported into cognitive psychology in the post-1970s explicitly through Anderson's Adaptive Control of Thought program. That is philosophy → psychology → architecture, not a parallel engineering discovery.
- Newell & Simon's ***Human Problem Solving* (1972)** is a *cognitive-psychology* monograph — its subtitle purpose was "theories of human psychological behavior," and their production systems fire when the IF-pattern matches the contents of **short-term memory** (their term, borrowed from the psychology of the day). Soar and ACT-R were built by people whose stated goal was modeling *human* cognition, inside the same cognitive-revolution community as Miller, Atkinson, and Shiffrin.
- CoALA is the most damning: the steelman itself concedes (in Claim B) that CoALA is "analogical by design," "mirroring cognitive-psychology distinctions." **A deliberate import cannot be counted as an independent convergence.** The partition shows up in the agent literature because Sumers et al. *read Tulving and Squire and chose to organize their survey that way.* Counting that as a third derivation is double-counting the psychology.

So the honest revision: this is **not** psychology + neuroscience + AI as three witnesses. It is **one intellectual lineage** (mid-century information-processing psychology) that seeded both the cognitive architectures and, decades later, CoALA. The "convergence" is autocorrelation.

**2. The working-vs-long-term "convergence" is a shared *metaphor*, and the arrow runs the wrong way.**
The steelman calls working-vs-long-term a "convergent architectural necessity, not a metaphor." But the modal model was *itself* the computer metaphor: Atkinson & Shiffrin (1968) modeled memory as "an information processing system, much like a computer," with sensory "buffers" and a "rehearsal buffer" — vocabulary lifted from **primary/secondary storage in von Neumann computer architecture**, which predates them. So the causal chain is: computer memory hierarchy → 1960s psychology (Atkinson-Shiffrin adopt store/buffer language) → cognitive architectures → CoALA re-labels a context window "working memory." That is not three fields independently hitting a truth; it is **one engineering idea (fast small store vs. slow large store) laundered through psychology and handed back to engineering under psychological names.** The fact that "any stream-processing system needs something fast and something durable" is true of a laptop, a CPU cache, and a filing cabinet — its universality makes it *nearly vacuous as evidence for a shared cognitive joint*, not deep.

**3. The double dissociation — the steelman's explicitly named "load-bearing evidence" — does not carry the weight placed on it.**
- *Logic:* Double dissociation does **not** entail separate systems/modules. **Dunn & Kirsner** (and the broader connectionist critique, e.g. Van Orden/Plaut) show that single-mechanism, distributed systems can and do produce double dissociations; the inference to "separable modules" is inference to the *best*, not the *only*, explanation. H.M. and K.C. are consistent with the partition; they do not force it over a graded/continuum account.
- *Scope:* Even granting the dissociations, H.M. licenses **declarative vs. procedural**; K.C. licenses **episodic vs. semantic**. Neither lesion doubly-dissociates a unitary "working memory" store from the rest — the working-memory box is imported on separate (and internally contested, see C) grounds. So the clinical evidence supports *some* plurality, but **not the specific four-box taxonomy CoALA borrows.** The steelman slides from "memory is plural" (warranted) to "this partition earns its keep as separable mechanisms" (warranted only for two specific cuts) to underwriting the four-store scheme (not warranted by the dissociations at all).
- *Cleanliness:* The dissociations are also less surgical than the tidy telling. H.M. showed *some* residual declarative learning; the developmental-amnesia cases (Vargha-Khadem) that are supposed to purify the episodic/semantic split are themselves contested by Squire & Zola. The "clean" double dissociation is a textbook idealization of a messier record.

**What resists in A:** That *memory is plural at the level of coarse functional dissociations* is genuinely robust and lesion-grounded — even the critics keep declarative/nondeclarative. And the declarative-vs-procedural cut does map onto a real, recurring computational cleavage. What does **not** resist is the claim of **independent tri-field convergence** and the claim that the dissociations validate the **particular four-box partition**. Downgrade A from "triply-grounded convergence" to "one lineage's idealization, coarsely dissociation-supported, deliberately re-imported by AI."

## CLAIM B — "productive analogy, not possession." Largely correct; the residual overreach is that it rests the verdict on a single, contested definition.

B is the strongest-reasoned section and I cannot break its conclusion. But its *sharpness* — "category error," "precisely not episodic memory" — is bought by silently selecting the **strongest** available definition of episodic memory and treating it as *the* definition.

**1. The autonoetic criterion is not consensus; it is Tulving's stipulation, and it is arguably untestable even in humans.** Autonoetic consciousness is inferred only from first-person verbal report (Tulving's Remember/Know paradigm). **Dunn (2004)** and others argue R/K judgments are explained by a single signal-detection memory-strength dimension — no two conscious states required. If the criterion that individuates episodic memory is itself a contested inference from introspective report, then "the agent lacks autonoetic consciousness" is not a crisp category boundary; it is *unfalsifiable on both sides*. B is right to withhold possession, but it should not claim the criterion is clean.

**2. Comparative cognition already abandoned that criterion — and did so deliberately, precisely because it is untestable in non-verbal systems.** Clayton & Dickinson (1998, *Nature*) coined **"episodic-like memory"** for scrub jays exactly because "behavioural tasks can never speak to the phenomenological experience of remembering (autonoetic consciousness)." They defined an **operational what-where-when** criterion instead. This matters enormously for B: under the *episodic-like* (behavioral WWW) definition that mainstream science uses for every organism that can't file a phenomenology report, a system that stores and correctly retrieves what/where/when *does* satisfy the working criterion. So the verdict "agents merely have the storage role, not episodic memory" is **definition-relative**: true under Tulving's autonoetic gold standard, blurry under the behavioral standard the field itself adopted when it needed a testable one. The steelman never confronts that it has picked the one definition guaranteed to exclude machines — the same move that would exclude scrub jays, rats, and arguably pre-verbal children.

**What resists in B:** The core claim is correct and important — labeling a store "episodic" names a function, and no current agent architecture provides *or is claimed by CoALA to provide* autonoetic re-experiencing; the very LLM-episodic-memory literature frames the capability as *missing/to-be-built*, which corroborates modesty. The false-equivalence charge lands cleanly: engineers imported the storage/retrieval structure and dropped the consciousness axis. I'd only sand off the overconfident "category error" and restate it as: *under Tulving's criterion it's analogy; under the behavioral criterion the boundary is genuinely fuzzy, and which you pick is a choice, not a fact.*

## CLAIM C — "systems-vs-continuum is contested." Straightforwardly true. The attack is that it *proves too much for the topic title.*

C is the best-supported claim and I verified its architecture is real: the **Norris (2017) → Cowan et al. (2019)** exchange in *Psychological Bulletin* is a live, top-journal dispute; **Cowan's embedded-processes / activated-LTM** model denies a separate STM store; **Cabeza & Moscovitch (2013)** frames systems/processing-modes/components as an unresolved three-way tension; **Sherry & Schacter (1987)** shows "what counts as a system" is a stipulation. These are load-bearing and they hold.

The red-team point is not that C is wrong — it's that C, taken seriously, **retroactively convicts the topic title and Claim A of overstatement.** The topic is stated as *"Memory is plural (the systems partition: working/episodic/semantic/procedural)"* — i.e., the four boxes presented as the established structure. C establishes that (i) the separate short-term store is disputed (Cowan), (ii) the episodic/semantic border is graded, and even **Tulving's own SPI model** makes episodic encoding *dependent* on semantic — so the boxes aren't modular at encoding, (iii) the "systems" framing is contested *by the neuroimaging that was supposed to vindicate it* (the components camp). So the honest consequence is that the defensible residue is **"coarse functional dissociations are robust; the four-box modular partition is an idealization with disputed seams"** — which is a materially *weaker* claim than the topic title asserts. The steelman's "this makes the baseline more rigorous" turn is correct but it is also a **concession**: it concedes that the partition-as-clean-fact is over-claimed, which is exactly the red-team verdict on A.

One sharpening the steelman under-plays: the SPI dependency and the Cowan activated-LTM view don't just contest *fine* structure — they contest whether "separate stores" is even the right ontology, versus **one substrate in different activation/processing states.** If working memory is activated LTM and episodic retrieval is an operation-at-retrieval rather than content in a dedicated subsystem, then the agent-side picture of *four distinct stores* is analogizing to a human architecture that a serious slice of the field says **doesn't exist as four stores.** That deepens the Claim-B false-equivalence: engineers may be mirroring a modular picture that is itself a contested idealization, not the settled science.
