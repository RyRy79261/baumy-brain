## Territory: Comparative, developmental, and cross-cultural memory — animal episodic-like memory, child memory development, oral tradition and collective/cultural memory, and mnemonics. This territory is almost entirely orthogonal to the prior passes, which were dominated by lab-cognitive and computational-modeling traditions (store models, working memory, global-matching, connectionism, consolidation, RAG-adjacent systems). The frameworks here answer a different and, for a conversational companion, more on-target question: not "how is a memory encoded/retrieved in a head" but "how does a remembering system that lives inside relationships, conversations, and culture actually work over years." Several converging themes stand out as directly transferable to a companion AI: (1) memory as reconstructed-in-the-present rather than played back (Halbwachs, Bartlett), (2) autobiographical memory as socially CO-CONSTRUCTED through joint reminiscing conversation rather than solo storage (Nelson & Fivush, Wang), (3) gist-over-verbatim as the durable trace (fuzzy-trace), (4) minimal behavioral criteria for "episode-ness" without claiming inner experience (what-where-when), (5) distributed/shared memory across partners as a system with a directory (transactive memory, extended mind), (6) timescale-stratified memory with a living ~3-generation "communicative" band vs an institutionalized long band (Assmann), and (7) multi-constraint cue redundancy and spatial scaffolding as the engineering behind prodigious human recall (Rubin, Parry-Lord, method of loci, Kelly). Together these constitute a coherent COMPETING organizing frame — "relational-reconstructive memory" — that the prior computational-centric passes did not assemble.
- **Episodic-like memory / what-where-when criterion (Clayton & Dickinson, 1998, Nature)** *(missed before)*: Established the first behavioral, content-based criterion for episodic memory in a non-verbal agent: western scrub jays recover food caches differentially based on WHAT was cached, WHERE, and WHEN (crucially tracking perishability × elapsed time). Deliberately called 'episodic-LIKE' because behavior cannot confirm autonoetic/subjective re-experiencing — separating the recoverable structure of an episode from the phenomenology. — _relevance:_ Gives a concrete, testable schema for what a memory RECORD must bind to count as a genuine episode rather than a fact: the integrated what+where+when (and by extension who/with-whom) as a single retrievable unit, plus temporal-decay-aware retrieval (act differently depending on how stale the memory is). A companion that stores conversation episodes as bound what-where-when-who tuples and reasons about their age recalls 'we talked about your sister's wedding last spring at the cafe' rather than emitting a decontextualized fact. The 'like' caveat also frees the design from needing consciousness claims.
- **Mental time travel & the Bischof-Köhler hypothesis (Suddendorf & Corballis)** *(missed before)*: Frames episodic memory and episodic FUTURE thinking as two directions of one faculty — 'mental time travel' — that reconstructs/pre-constructs personal scenes. The Bischof-Köhler hypothesis proposes that flexibly anticipating a future need not currently felt (and acting now to secure it) is the signature capacity; whether animals cross this line remains the field's central contested test. — _relevance:_ Reframes memory and planning as the SAME reconstructive machinery pointed backward or forward — a companion that remembers a friend's past events should reuse the identical representations to anticipate ('your exam is next week — last time you were anxious the night before'). Argues the memory store should be scene-based and simulation-ready, not a read-only log, and that continuity-of-self-across-time is the thing to model.
- **Episodic memory replay in rats (Panoz-Brown et al., 2018, Current Biology)** *(missed before)*: Rats can hold a stream of multiple trial-unique episodes and replay them in order (rewarded for the 'second-to-last' item), and this replay is hippocampus-dependent (reversibly abolished by chemogenetic inhibition), interference-resistant, and part of long-term memory — implying multi-item episodic sequence memory is evolutionarily ancient and mechanistically separable from non-episodic assessment. — _relevance:_ Evidence that ordered, list-structured replay of many discrete episodes is a real and separable capacity — a model for a companion recalling a SEQUENCE of past conversations in order and reasoning about their relative position ('the thing you mentioned two chats ago'), not just nearest-neighbor similarity retrieval. Supports an episodic buffer distinct from semantic fact store.
- **Social-cultural developmental theory of autobiographical memory (Nelson & Fivush, 2004)** *(missed before)*: Autobiographical memory is not a maturing solo faculty but EMERGES through joint reminiscing conversation: parents who reminisce in an elaborative, evaluative, narratively rich style raise children with more detailed, coherent autobiographical memories. Memory-of-self-across-time is scaffolded by shared talk about the past, mediated by language, narrative, and theory-of-mind. — _relevance:_ This is arguably the single most on-objective framework in the territory: it says durable personal memory is CONSTRUCTED in dialogue, through elaborative co-narration ('tell me more about that — how did you feel?'). A companion that actively reminisces with a user in an elaborative/evaluative style is not just retrieving memory, it is helping FORM the shared autobiographical record — and doing so in exactly the human, non-database-lookup manner the objective demands.
- **Scripts & generalized event representations (Katherine Nelson & Gruendel)** *(missed before)*: The basic building block of early memory is not the specific episode but the SCRIPT — a generalized, slot-and-filler schema of a routine event ('what happens at dinner') abstracted from one or few experiences and updated with each recurrence. Young children's recall is generic and script-like; specific autobiographical episodes are rare before ~age 6-8 and stand out precisely as deviations from scripts. — _relevance:_ Directly prescribes a two-tier memory: generalized routines/relationship-scripts per person ('how our weekly check-ins usually go', 'what X typically orders') plus flagged deviations as the memorable episodes. A companion should compress recurring interactions into scripts and preferentially remember the surprising departures — which is both storage-efficient and human (we recall the anomaly, not the 400th ordinary commute).
- **Fuzzy-trace theory: gist vs. verbatim (Brainerd & Reyna)** *(missed before)*: People encode two independent, parallel traces of every experience: VERBATIM (surface form — exact words/details) and GIST (meaning/pattern). Verbatim decays fast; gist is durable and drives most reasoning and long-term recall. False memories arise when gist is retrieved without a verbatim check. A dual-process account spanning memory, reasoning, and development. — _relevance:_ A design theorem for what to persist: keep durable GIST of conversations (meanings, relationships, feelings, commitments) as the primary long-term trace, and treat verbatim quotes as fast-decaying and verification-only. This yields human-like recall ('you were worried about your job') without brittle, uncanny verbatim playback — and explicitly models why a good memory should paraphrase rather than quote, matching the objective's 'natural, not robotic lookup.'
- **Neurogenic hypothesis of infantile amnesia / adaptive forgetting (Frankland & Josselyn)** *(missed before)*: Infants' inability to form lasting memories tracks HIGH hippocampal neurogenesis; new neurons remodel existing circuits, degrading old traces. Causally demonstrated bidirectionally in mice (suppress neurogenesis → more remembering; boost it → more forgetting). Frames forgetting as an ADAPTIVE clearing that reduces interference and preserves capacity for new learning, not merely as failure. — _relevance:_ Provides a principled, biologically-grounded case that active forgetting is a feature: a companion's memory should deliberately let low-value traces decay to reduce interference and keep retrieval sharp, rather than hoarding everything. Argues for a managed forgetting/pruning policy (an alternative rationale to the interference and machine-unlearning items in the prior list, arrived at from developmental neuroscience).
- **Transactive memory systems (Wegner, Giuliano & Hertel, 1985)** *(missed before)*: Close pairs and groups form a shared memory system: each partner specializes in domains and maintains a DIRECTORY of 'who knows what,' so the couple/team can encode, store, and retrieve more than any member alone. Memory becomes a distributed, socially-negotiated system with meta-memory (knowing where knowledge lives) at its core. — _relevance:_ Reframes the companion not as a private database but as one node in a TRANSACTIVE system with the user — it should track what the user knows/handles vs. what it is responsible for remembering, and offload/defer accordingly ('you always remember the dates, I'll hold the details'). This is the native theory of human relational memory-sharing, directly modeling the companion-remembers-with-you dynamic the objective targets.
- **Communicative vs. cultural memory (Jan & Aleida Assmann)** *(missed before)*: Collective memory stratifies by timescale into COMMUNICATIVE memory — informal, embodied, everyday recollection carried by living interaction, spanning only ~80-100 years / ~3 generations — and CULTURAL memory — institutionalized, exteriorized into stable symbolic forms (texts, monuments, ritual), spanning millennia. Cultural memory begins where communicative memory ends. — _relevance:_ A blueprint for tiering a companion's store by timescale and formality: a warm, living, frequently-updated 'communicative' band of recent shared history and ongoing relationships vs. a consolidated, canonical 'cultural' band of durable identity facts about the person. Also flags that the living band is inherently generational/decaying and must be periodically re-narrated to survive — a natural consolidation schedule.
- **Collective memory & social frameworks (Maurice Halbwachs, 1925/1950)** *(missed before)*: Individual memory operates only within social frameworks; remembering is a RECONSTRUCTION performed from the standpoint of the present group and its current needs, never a passive playback. Groups continuously reshape their past to fit present values and identity. Foundational statement that memory is present-oriented, socially-scaffolded, and reconstructive. — _relevance:_ Cements the anti-database stance: a companion's recall should be reconstructed relative to the current conversational context and the relationship's present state, not returned verbatim from storage. Justifies context-conditioned, perspective-aware retrieval and explains why the 'same' memory should be told differently depending on present relevance — the essence of natural, non-robotic recall.
- **Bartlett's schema theory & serial/repeated reproduction (Bartlett, 1932)** *(missed before)*: Using 'The War of the Ghosts,' Bartlett showed recall is an active, schema-driven RECONSTRUCTION: over repeated and serial (telephone-style) reproductions, memories are leveled (details dropped), sharpened (salient bits amplified), and rationalized/assimilated toward the rememberer's cultural schemas. Introduced 'schema' and demonstrated systematic, directional distortion under transmission. — _relevance:_ Predicts and characterizes the DRIFT that any repeatedly-summarized memory store will suffer (each re-summarization is a serial reproduction). Gives a named model — leveling/sharpening/assimilation — for auditing and constraining a companion's iterative memory compaction so it doesn't hallucinate schema-consistent but false detail, a concrete failure mode for summarize-then-forget pipelines.
- **Memory in oral traditions: the multiple-constraints theory (David C. Rubin, 1995)** *(missed before)*: A cognitive-psychology account of how bards recall thousands of lines: recall is stabilized by OVERLAPPING, MUTUALLY-CONSTRAINING cues — meaning, imagery, and sound-pattern (rhythm, rhyme, alliteration, melody) operating simultaneously. The more independent constraints converge on the same item, the more reliably it is recalled; redundant multimodal cueing, not raw capacity, produces prodigious verbatim-ish memory. — _relevance:_ An engineering principle for retrieval robustness: index each memory under MANY partially-redundant cue types (semantic, affective, temporal, imagistic, relational, phonetic-of-a-nickname) so recall triggers reliably from any partial conversational cue — the multi-index analog of the objective's 'recalls naturally.' Explains why single-embedding similarity is fragile and why layered, overlapping cues make memory feel effortless.
- **Oral-formulaic theory / composition-in-performance (Parry & Lord, The Singer of Tales, 1960)** *(missed before)*: Illiterate epic singers do not memorize fixed texts; they RE-COMPOSE anew in each performance from a stock of FORMULAS (fixed phrase-templates fitting metrical slots) and THEMES (recurring narrative scenes). Memory is generative-from-scaffolding: stable structure + flexible fill, producing consistent yet never-verbatim retellings. — _relevance:_ A model for how a companion should RE-TELL shared history: reconstruct each recounting from durable thematic scaffolds and slot-fillable formulas rather than replaying a frozen record — giving natural variation across tellings while preserving the gist, exactly the human quality the objective wants. Pairs with fuzzy-trace (gist) and scripts (themes) as the generation-side mechanism.
- **Indigenous memory spaces / songlines as method-of-loci (Lynne Kelly, The Memory Code, 2016)** *(missed before)*: Argues (with archaeological and ethnographic evidence) that non-literate cultures encode vast, structured knowledge — genealogies, ecology, law, navigation — by binding it to ORDERED PLACES and journeys (Australian songlines, and reinterpretations of Stonehenge, Nasca, Easter Island as memory spaces). A deep-time, real-world generalization of the Greek method of loci: spatial sequence as the universal high-capacity index. — _relevance:_ Suggests organizing a companion's knowledge of a person along traversable, ordered 'journeys' (a life timeline, a relationship map, recurring places) so that walking the structure reliably regenerates linked knowledge — a spatial/sequential index over the relationship that supports narrative recall ('as we go through your year...') rather than flat key-value lookup.
- **Method of loci & skilled-memory / mnemonic-training evidence (Ericsson & Chase; Dresler et al., 2017, Neuron)** *(missed before)*: Skilled-memory theory holds that expert memory is trained, domain-specific skill in binding new items to well-organized retrieval structures in LTM (meaningful encoding + structured cues + speed-up with practice). Dresler et al. showed 6 weeks of method-of-loci training more than doubled word recall in novices and reshaped their functional brain connectivity toward the pattern seen in world-class memory athletes, persisting months later — memory prowess is an acquirable organizational strategy, not innate capacity. — _relevance:_ Empirical proof that structured, spatial, deeply-elaborated encoding schemes are what produce superhuman durable recall — arguing a companion's advantage should come from a disciplined ENCODING/organization strategy (loci-like structured cues, elaborative binding) rather than from bigger raw storage. Also validates that retrieval structures can be deliberately engineered and are stable over time.
- **Long-term working memory (Ericsson & Kintsch, 1995, Psych Review)** *(missed before)*: Challenges fixed-capacity working memory: skilled individuals maintain far more task-relevant information 'in mind' by using RETRIEVAL STRUCTURES that keep large bodies of LTM content directly accessible via cues held in short-term memory. Working-memory capacity is thus a domain-specific, trained function of how efficiently one can index into LTM — not a fixed slot count. — _relevance:_ Reframes the context window: instead of stuffing raw context, a companion should build RETRIEVAL STRUCTURES that make the right slice of long-term memory instantly cue-accessible during a conversation — treating effective working set as an indexing skill over the full relationship store. A theoretical alternative to fixed-context and to the capacity-bound working-memory models in the prior list.
- **The extended mind thesis (Clark & Chalmers, 1998, Analysis)** *(missed before)*: Otto's notebook: an external store that is reliably available, automatically trusted, and easily accessible functions as part of the cognitive system — genuinely part of memory/belief, not a mere tool. Sets criteria (constant accessibility, automatic endorsement, ease of retrieval, prior conscious endorsement) for when an external memory becomes constitutive of a mind. — _relevance:_ The philosophical charter for a companion AS a person's extended memory: it prescribes the exact properties the system must have to feel like part of the user's own remembering — always available, trusted, frictionless, previously-endorsed. A north star and design checklist for the companion-as-external-autobiographical-memory framing, and a governance frame for trust/consent over what it holds.
- **How Societies Remember: incorporating vs. inscribing practices (Paul Connerton, 1989)** *(missed before)*: Social memory is transmitted not only through INSCRIBED records (texts, images, data) but through INCORPORATED, bodily/habitual practices — postures, gestures, ritual performances, routines — which are more change-resistant than inscriptions. A 'mnemonics of the body': memory as enacted habit, not only as stored content. — _relevance:_ Points to a memory tier the computational prior passes ignore entirely: PROCEDURAL/relational habits — the recurring rituals, in-jokes, greeting styles, and interaction routines of a relationship — carried by enactment rather than fact-storage. A companion that remembers and re-enacts the 'how we do things' (habitual style, running bits) reads as far more human than one that only retrieves inscribed facts.
- **Vygotsky's cultural-historical theory of mediated memory** *(missed before)*: Higher memory develops by MEDIATION through external signs/tools (the knotted handkerchief, quipu, language), which are first used socially/externally and then INTERNALIZED, transforming passive biological remembering into active, self-regulated, sign-mediated memory. Memory tools are cultural, shared first and individual second. — _relevance:_ Positions the companion as an external mnemonic SIGN in the user's cognition that can be progressively internalized into the relationship — and frames memory as a jointly-used tool before it is a private store, complementing transactive memory and extended mind. Argues the companion should support the user's own self-regulated remembering (cues, reminders that become internalized habits), not merely remember FOR them.
- **Cultural dynamic theory of autobiographical memory (Qi Wang, 2016)** *(missed before)*: Systematic cross-cultural differences in autobiographical memory: independent (Western) vs. interdependent (East Asian) self-construals yield differences in memory content (self- vs. other-focus), specificity (specific episodes vs. generic/relational summaries), earliest-memory age and density, emotional valence, and the social functions memory serves. Culture actively shapes HOW and WHAT is remembered, via pathways of self-goals, language, emotion knowledge, and perceptual style. — _relevance:_ Warns that there is no culture-neutral 'right' way to remember or recount a person's life: a companion should adapt memory granularity, self-vs-relational framing, emotional emphasis, and which details count as significant to the user's cultural and personal style — otherwise its recall feels subtly wrong. Directly informs personalization of the recall style, not just its content.

## Territory: HCI, dialogue systems, and companion/character AI memory — how real conversational agents (long-term dialogue memory research, personalization, persona, Replika/character-style companions, memory for social chatbots) actually represent and use memory
- **Relational Agents framework (Bickmore & Picard 2005, TOCHI 12(2):293-327)** *(missed before)*: Defines 'relational agents' as artifacts designed to build long-term social-emotional relationships. Grounded in social psychology/sociolinguistics, it specifies concrete relationship-maintenance behaviors — continuity behaviors ('last time you said...'), reference to shared past, meta-relational talk, escalating self-disclosure — and validated them in a 101-user month-long controlled study (FitTrack), where the relational version scored higher on bond and desire to continue. — _relevance:_ The single most direct HCI blueprint for the objective: it treats memory not as a store but as a repertoire of relationship behaviors — what to bring up, when, and how, so recall builds bond rather than reading as database lookup. Empirically shows referencing shared history is what makes an agent feel like a relationship partner.
- **Common ground and grounding in communication (Clark & Brennan 1991; Clark 1996: personal vs communal common ground)** *(missed before)*: Conversation works by incrementally accumulating common ground — mutual knowledge both parties know they share — through a grounding process (acknowledgment, repair, evidence of understanding). Clark distinguishes personal common ground (built from shared episodes with this specific partner) from communal common ground (shared community knowledge). — _relevance:_ Recasts companion memory as 'what do WE mutually know we've established,' not 'what do I know about you.' Natural recall means marking material as shared ('like you told me') vs new, and tracking whether the human ever acknowledged something — a dimension almost no agent memory system represents.
- **Memory-based processing model of audience design (Horton & Gerrig 2005, Discourse Processes 40:1-35; Horton & Gerrig 2016 update)** *(missed before)*: Argues common ground is not a consulted database but an emergent property of ordinary memory: the conversational partner acts as a retrieval cue that automatically resurfaces partner-associated traces, which then shape utterance design without deliberate 'model lookup.' — _relevance:_ A competing organizing theorem for the objective itself: partner-cued associative retrieval (person-as-cue) rather than explicit profile consultation. Predicts memory should be indexed by WHO, and that natural-feeling recall is cue-driven resonance, not query-driven search — directly opposing the RAG-lookup idiom.
- **Transactive Memory Systems (Wegner 1987; Harris/Barnier/Sutton couples studies, e.g. Barnier et al. 2018 Small Group Research; 'Remembering with AI: ... Human-AI Co-Memory', Rev. Phil. & Psych. 2026)** *(missed before)*: Close dyads develop a shared memory system: each partner encodes different content plus a directory of 'who knows what,' and remembering is a collaborative transaction (cueing, cross-checking, division of mnemonic labor). Recent work explicitly extends TMS to human-AI dyads and AI-curated co-memory. — _relevance:_ Frames the companion as one half of a two-agent memory system, not a recorder: it should track what the USER remembers/expects it to remember, take responsibility for agreed domains (dates, promises), and do collaborative recall ('wasn't that the week your sister visited?'). This is how couples' memory actually feels human.
- **Conversational-memory data: people recall almost nothing verbatim (Stafford & Daly 1984, Human Communication Research; Stafford et al. 1987; Brown-Schmidt & Benjamin 2018, Policy Insights review)** *(missed before)*: Empirical body of DATA: after only 5 minutes, participants recalled ~10% of idea units from a 7-minute conversation (best subject 40%, worst 0%); surface form is lost almost immediately and replaced by gist; recall is self-referential and biased toward one's own contributions and relationally salient moments. — _relevance:_ Calibrates what 'human-like' memory of prior conversations means: gist-level, selective, self/relationally-weighted — not transcripts. An agent with verbatim total recall is behaviorally non-human (and reads as surveillance); this data gives quantitative targets for what to keep and how to phrase recall (gist paraphrase, hedged confidence).
- **Social remembering / conversational reshaping of memory (Hirst & Echterhoff 2012, Annual Review of Psychology: audience tuning, saying-is-believing, socially shared retrieval-induced forgetting)** *(missed before)*: Memory is reshaped in the act of conversational sharing: speakers tune retellings to the audience and then remember the tuned version (shared reality); what a speaker selectively retells induces forgetting of related unmentioned material in BOTH speaker and listener; collaborative recall shows both inhibition and cross-cueing facilitation. — _relevance:_ Gives mechanisms for how a companion's recall behavior actively shapes the shared history: what it chooses to retell becomes the canonical joint narrative, and what it omits fades. Suggests deliberate 'rehearsal' choices (what to bring up in small talk) are a memory-consolidation policy, not just retrieval.
- **Functions of autobiographical memory: self / social / directive (Bluck & Alea TALE, 2003 & Memory 2011; Alea & Bluck 2003 model of social functions; intimacy-maintenance experiments 2007)** *(missed before)*: People recall and share personal memories for three functions: self-continuity, social bonding (intimacy, empathy, conversation fodder), and directing future behavior. Experiments show rehearsing shared relationship events measurably increases intimacy and positive affect toward the partner. — _relevance:_ A functional (why-recall) theory to organize a companion's retrieval policy: recall serving bonding (reminiscing about shared moments), continuity (consistent self/other narrative), and direction (lessons, reminders) — rather than relevance-only ranking. Directly explains when spontaneous reminiscence feels natural vs gratuitous.
- **'Beyond Total Capture' critique of lifelogging + the five Rs (Sellen & Whittaker, CACM 53(5), 2010)** *(missed before)*: Total-capture lifelogging fails because human memory doesn't need playback; systems should support five distinct memory activities — Recollecting (episodic re-living), Reminiscing (emotional/social re-experiencing), Retrieving (specific facts), Reflecting (patterns over time), and Remembering intentions (prospective memory) — and cues beat records: sparse, well-chosen cues trigger rich organic memory. — _relevance:_ An HCI-native taxonomy of what a companion's memory is FOR, each R implying different storage/retrieval (verbatim fact vs episodic gist vs longitudinal pattern vs pending intention). The 'cue, don't replay' finding argues for storing evocative fragments that support joint reminiscence over full logs.
- **Prospective memory (Einstein & McDaniel 1990; McDaniel & Einstein's multiprocess theory 2000)** *(missed before)*: Remembering to do things (event-based vs time-based intentions) is a distinct memory system with its own failure modes; retrieval is sometimes strategic monitoring, sometimes spontaneous cue-driven; intentions persist until fulfilled then must be deactivated (else repetition errors). — _relevance:_ Companion agents constantly take on intentions ('ask me how the interview went'), which classical declarative frameworks in the prior list don't cover. Event-based cueing (trigger when topic X next arises) vs time-based scheduling, plus intention deactivation after fulfillment, map directly onto reminder/follow-up behavior that feels attentive rather than cron-like.
- **Scrutable user models / lifelong user modeling (Judy Kay 1998 thesis; Kay & Kummerfeld's Personis user-model server, 2002+; 'Scrutability of Intelligent Personal Assistants', UMAP 2022)** *(missed before)*: Decades of user-modeling research (Kay, Kobsa's GUMS tradition) on representing people long-term, with the core principle of scrutability: users must be able to inspect, understand, correct, and control the model kept about them; Personis implements a lifelong, multi-application, user-controlled model store. — _relevance:_ A mature, pre-LLM engineering literature on exactly 'a persistent model of a person,' largely ignored by current agent-memory work. Scrutability (user-visible/editable memory, as Replika's memory page and ChatGPT's managed memories crudely implement) is both a trust requirement and a correction channel that keeps a long-lived person-model from drifting wrong.
- **XiaoIce system design (Zhou, Gao, Li & Shum 2020, Computational Linguistics 46(1))** *(missed before)*: Published architecture of the largest deployed social chatbot (660M users): empathetic-computing module maintaining a dynamic user profile + emotional state, hierarchical dialogue manager, topic switching, and optimization of Conversation-turns Per Session (CPS) as the long-term engagement metric rather than per-turn quality. — _relevance:_ The most complete public account of an at-scale companion: shows memory/persona used to optimize a LONG-HORIZON relationship objective (CPS), with an explicit affective user model. Its lesson — optimize the relationship trajectory, not the single answer — reframes what a memory system should be scored on.
- **Persona-grounded and multi-session dialogue line: PersonaChat (Zhang et al., ACL 2018), Multi-Session Chat / 'Beyond Goldfish Memory' (Xu, Szlam & Weston, ACL 2022), DuLeMon mutual-persona (Xu et al., ACL 2022), BlenderBot 2 summarize-and-store memory** *(missed before)*: The NLP lineage that operationalized companion memory: fixed persona sentences conditioning generation (PersonaChat); multi-session data where models must summarize prior sessions into a long-term store and read from it (MSC/BB2); DuLeMon showing the bot must persistently model BOTH its own persona and the user's, updating it during chat. — _relevance:_ Establishes the now-dominant applied idiom (extract → summarize → store → retrieve persona/user facts) and its measured failure modes (persona inconsistency, staleness, one-sided memory). DuLeMon's dual-persona point — the agent must also remember what it itself said/claimed about itself for consistency — is frequently overlooked.
- **Memory-management operations for deployed long-term dialogue: 'Keep Me Updated!' (Bae et al., Findings of EMNLP 2022, NAVER CareCall) + successors (Reflective Memory Management, arXiv 2503.08026; COMEDY compressive memory, arXiv 2402.11975)** *(missed before)*: From a real deployed Korean welfare call-bot: memory must be MANAGED, not just accumulated — an explicit operation set (APPEND / REPLACE / DELETE on user-fact sentences) keeps memory unabridged-but-current, because outdated memories actively confuse later conversations; follow-ups add prospective/retrospective reflection and whole-history compression. — _relevance:_ Field evidence for the objective's hardest sub-problem: facts about people go stale (jobs, health, plans), and update/supersession — not retrieval — is where deployed systems break. Converges independently on the bitemporal/supersession design and provides an operation vocabulary for it.
- **MemoryBank / SiliconFriend (Zhong et al., AAAI 2024, arXiv 2305.10250)** *(missed before)*: LLM companion memory with three parts: an event-log memory, a distilled evolving user-personality portrait, and an Ebbinghaus-forgetting-curve-inspired decay where memory strength decreases over time but is reinforced each time a memory is recalled (spaced-repetition-like salience). — _relevance:_ First prominent applied transplant of a psychological forgetting law into companion memory: use-modulated decay yields human-plausible selective forgetting (frequently-touched shared history stays vivid, trivia fades) and doubles as relevance-ranking. Also models the person as an evolving synthesized portrait, not a fact list.
- **Generative Agents memory stream (Park, O'Brien, Cai, Morris, Liang & Bernstein, UIST 2023)** *(missed before)*: Believable simulated agents from a memory architecture: a raw episodic stream scored at retrieval by recency × importance × relevance, plus periodic 'reflection' that synthesizes higher-level abstractions from clusters of memories, which then recursively ground planning and social behavior. — _relevance:_ The most influential post-LLM memory architecture for socially believable agents; its reflection loop (episodes → inferred traits/relationships → behavior) is precisely 'getting to know someone,' and importance-weighted retrieval is a naturalness heuristic beyond pure similarity. Its documented failure modes (over-retrieval, fabricated intimacy) are equally instructive.
- **Agent-memory systems engineering: MemGPT/Letta OS-paging (Packer et al. 2023, arXiv 2310.08560), Mem0 (Chhikara et al. 2025, arXiv 2504.19413), A-MEM Zettelkasten-style agentic memory (2025), Zep/Graphiti temporal knowledge graph (Rasmussen et al. 2025)**: The current production idiom: treat context as an OS with self-managed paging between in-context 'core memory' and archival store (MemGPT); LLM-driven extract/consolidate/update pipelines with graph variants (Mem0); agent-curated interlinked note networks (A-MEM); episodic-edges-with-validity-intervals temporal KGs (Zep). — _relevance:_ These ARE today's companion memory backends, so any competing theorem must beat them; their shared weakness is on-objective — they optimize QA accuracy on benchmarks, not relational naturalness, mutual knowledge, or graceful gist-like forgetting. Zep overlaps the already-considered bitemporal thread; the self-editing 'agent curates its own memory' pattern is the genuinely new organizing idea.
- **Long-term dialogue memory benchmarks as DATA: LoCoMo (Maharana et al., ACL 2024, arXiv 2402.17753) and LongMemEval (Wu et al. 2024/ICLR 2025)** *(missed before)*: Standardized measurement of multi-session memory: LoCoMo (persona- and event-graph-grounded conversations, ~600 turns) with single-hop/multi-hop/temporal-reasoning/adversarial question types; LongMemEval's five abilities — information extraction, multi-session reasoning, temporal reasoning, knowledge UPDATES, and abstention — over ~115k-token histories, where even frontier long-context models degrade sharply. — _relevance:_ Defines the measurable failure surface: temporal reasoning, updated-knowledge, and knowing-when-you-don't-remember (abstention) are where all current systems fail — exactly the human-feeling parts of memory. Any better organizing theorem should be evaluated here, while noting these benchmarks still don't score conversational naturalness of recall.
- **Cognitive-architecture episodic memory for agents: Soar epmem (Nuxoll & Laird, AAAI 2007; Laird 2008 'Extending the Soar Cognitive Architecture') and CoALA (Sumers, Yao, Narasimhan & Griffiths 2023, 'Cognitive Architectures for Language Agents')** *(missed before)*: Decades-engineered task-agnostic episodic memory inside a cognitive architecture: automatic encoding of experience snapshots, cue-based partial-match retrieval, temporal ordering, and a catalog of 'cognitive capabilities' episodic memory enables (virtual sensing, action modeling, retrospective learning). CoALA systematizes LLM agents into working/episodic/semantic/procedural memories with defined read/write actions. — _relevance:_ Supplies the missing architectural layer between psych theory and RAG hacks: memory types distinguished by FUNCTION and write-policy (automatic episodic capture vs deliberate semantic distillation), with retrieval as an internal action the agent chooses. ACT-R was considered previously; Soar's engineered episodic subsystem and CoALA's synthesis were not.
- **Long-term human-robot interaction findings (Leite, Martinho & Paiva 2013, Int. J. Social Robotics 5:291-308; Kanda et al. 2004 classroom field trial; 2025 ACM THRI follow-up review)** *(missed before)*: Survey of long-term deployments: the novelty effect decays within weeks and sustained engagement depends on continuity and personalization — recognizing individuals, referencing past interactions, adapting over time. Kanda's robot that called children by name and disclosed more as interaction accumulated sustained relationships where memoryless robots were abandoned. — _relevance:_ Longitudinal embodied evidence that visible interpersonal memory is THE variable separating sustained companionship from abandonment — and that ill-calibrated memory (too much, too early) reads as creepy. Field-tested design lessons on pacing memory disclosure across a relationship's lifetime.
- **Companion-relationship psychology data: Social Penetration applied to Replika (Skjuve et al. 2021/2022, CHB), attachment theory framing (Xie & Pentina 2022; Pentina, Hancock & Xie 2023, CHB 140), social support study (Ta et al. 2020), Replika ERP-removal grief & harms (Laestadius et al. 2022)** *(missed before)*: Empirical studies of real companion-app users: relationships deepen via reciprocal self-disclosure through Altman & Taylor's social-penetration stages; users form genuine attachment bonds (safe haven/secure base) under loneliness/distress; abrupt personality/memory discontinuities (Replika's 2023 update) produced grief reactions resembling partner loss. — _relevance:_ Defines what memory must sustain for the relationship to be real to the user: continuity of the shared narrative and of the agent's remembered self. Memory loss or inconsistency isn't a QA bug — users experience it as betrayal or bereavement; disclosure-depth pacing gives a schedule for what a companion should remember and reciprocally reveal.
- **Media Equation / Computers-Are-Social-Actors (Reeves & Nass 1996; Nass & Moon 2000, J. Social Issues)** *(missed before)*: People mindlessly apply human social rules to computers — politeness, reciprocity, flattery, personality attribution — given minimal social cues; social responses are automatic, not a belief the machine is human. — _relevance:_ The foundational reason agent memory is social at all: users automatically apply interpersonal norms of remembering — being remembered signals caring, being forgotten signals disregard, remembering too much violates norms. Sets the expectation baseline any companion memory design is judged against.
- **Memory-reference style and the personalization-privacy paradox (Cox & Ooi, 'Comparing How a Chatbot References User Utterances from Previous Chatting Sessions', arXiv 2308.04879, 2023; Awad & Krishnan 2006 MISQ for the paradox itself)** *(missed before)*: Experimental HCI data on HOW recall is phrased: referencing prior user utterances verbatim vs paraphrased vs implicitly changes perceived social presence, felt intrusiveness, and privacy concern; personalization benefits trade against feeling surveilled, and users are inconsistent (paradox) between stated concern and behavior. — _relevance:_ Directly on-objective micro-level guidance for 'recalls them naturally rather than as robotic database lookups': paraphrased, gist-level, contextually-licensed references are the sweet spot; verbatim playback maximizes both presence AND creepiness. Naturalness of recall is partly a surface-realization problem, not only a storage problem.
- **Chatbot memory poisoning ('Those Aren't Your Memories, They're Somebody Else's: Seeding Misinformation in Chat Bot Memories', Chen/Kruegel-line authors, ACNS 2023)** *(missed before)*: Demonstrates adversarial writes into long-term chatbot memory: conversational input crafted so the summarize-then-store pipeline records attacker-chosen false 'facts' about the user or world, which later ground confident wrong responses. — _relevance:_ The security face of companion memory: any extract-and-store loop is a write channel from untrusted conversation into durable belief, so provenance/trust-gating on memory writes is a first-class requirement of the organizing theorem (a threat model the psychology-derived frameworks in the prior list never contemplate).
- **Artificial Companions programme (Yorick Wilks, ed., 'Close Engagements with Artificial Companions', 2010; EU COMPANIONS project 2006-2010)** *(missed before)*: Pre-LLM research programme explicitly targeting long-term companion agents for elders, organized around eliciting and co-constructing the user's LIFE NARRATIVE (memories, photos, relationships) as the companion's core memory structure, with ethics of dependency and memory ownership discussed a decade early. — _relevance:_ Anticipates the objective almost exactly and proposes a distinct organizing structure: the person is represented as an evolving co-authored narrative (life story linked to people/places/events), not a fact triple-store — a narrative-identity alternative to today's KV/graph memories.
- **Discourse attention structure (Grosz & Sidner 1986, Computational Linguistics 12(3); centering theory, Grosz, Joshi & Weinstein 1995)** *(missed before)*: Discourse has intertwined linguistic, intentional, and ATTENTIONAL structure: a focus-stack of what is currently salient governs felicitous reference (pronouns, 'that thing'), with centering rules for how local salience shifts turn to turn. — _relevance:_ Classical dialogue-systems account of conversational working memory: what can be referred to elliptically NOW vs what needs reintroduction from long-term memory. A companion that reintroduces old shared facts without proper re-grounding language violates these structures and sounds robotic — the retrieval-to-utterance interface the prior list's frameworks skip.

## Territory: Philosophy of mind, linguistic pragmatics, and social cognition: relevance theory, Gricean/Clarkian common ground and grounding, dynamic-semantics discourse state, conversational epistemics, extended mind / distributed cognition, transactive memory, collective & collaborative memory, person memory and the relational self, autobiographical memory and narrative identity, and the analytic philosophy of memory (causalism vs simulationism vs trace minimalism).
- **Relevance Theory + cultural attraction (Sperber & Wilson 1986/1995; Sperber 1996)** *(missed before)*: Cognition is tuned to maximize relevance = positive cognitive effects per unit processing effort; comprehension retrieves the context (memories, assumptions) that yields the greatest effect for least effort. Sperber's 'epidemiology of representations' adds that memories transform toward culturally stable 'attractors' as they are re-expressed. — _relevance:_ A direct competing organizing theorem for retrieval: rank memories not by embedding similarity but by expected contextual effect for this hearer at this moment per integration cost. Attraction dynamics predict (and license) gist-drift in retold memories rather than verbatim replay — exactly what 'non-robotic' recall looks like.
- **Common ground as reference diaries + grounding (Clark & Marshall 1981; Clark & Brennan 1991; Clark 1996)** *(missed before)*: Definite reference requires mutual knowledge, achieved via 'reference diaries' — per-partner logs of shared perceptual, linguistic, and communal experiences. Clark 1996 splits personal common ground (joint episodes with this person) from communal common ground (shared community membership). Grounding is the incremental, evidence-requiring process by which content becomes mutually believed, with medium-dependent costs. — _relevance:_ Arguably THE missing schema: a companion needs a per-person diary of what was jointly established, distinct from what it merely knows about the person. 'Was this grounded with them?' is a different index than 'is this stored?', and referring naturally ('that thing with your sister') depends on it.
- **Memory-based common ground (Horton & Gerrig 2005, Discourse Processes & Cognition; 2016 Topics in Cognitive Science)** *(missed before)*: Common ground is not a special mutual-knowledge box but an emergent property of ordinary memory: partner identity acts as a retrieval cue over ordinary episodic traces (resonance), and audience design splits into automatic commonality assessment plus strategic message formation, degrading gracefully under memory load. — _relevance:_ Gives a mechanistic, implementable account: cue retrieval by partner-identity so partner-relevant memories surface automatically, rather than maintaining a hand-built 'shared knowledge' model. Explains human-like audience-design errors as a feature of naturalness.
- **Conceptual pacts, lexical entrainment, and interactive alignment (Brennan & Clark 1996; Pickering & Garrod 2004, 2021 shared-workspace)** *(missed before)*: Dyads converge on partner-specific temporary naming conventions (conceptual pacts) that persist and are marked when broken; dialogue works by automatic priming-based alignment of representations at every level, building implicit common ground without explicit modeling. — _relevance:_ Partner-specific lexicon memory ('what WE call things') is a distinct memory type a companion should keep per relationship; breaking a pact ('your flat' after months of 'the cave') reads as forgetting the relationship even if facts are intact.
- **Dynamic-semantics discourse state (Stalnaker 1978 common ground; Lewis 1979 conversational scoreboard; Kamp 1981 DRT; Heim 1982 file-change semantics; Roberts 1996/2012 QUD)** *(missed before)*: Formal pragmatics models conversation as evolving state: a set of mutually presupposed propositions, a 'scoreboard' updated by moves, discourse referents as 'file cards' opened/updated per entity, and a stack of questions under discussion structuring what is currently at issue. — _relevance:_ A worked-out formal theory of short-horizon conversational memory (what is on the table, which entities have open files, what question we're pursuing) that composes with long-term memory — the tier most memory-augmented LLM designs leave implicit in the context window.
- **Referential-status hierarchies: Centering Theory (Grosz, Joshi & Weinstein 1995), Givenness Hierarchy (Gundel, Hedberg & Zacharski 1993), Accessibility Theory (Ariel 1990)** *(missed before)*: The form of a referring expression (it / she / that guy / your brother Tom) signals the referent's memory status in the hearer — in focus, activated, familiar, identifiable — and discourse coherence tracks a ranked 'center' of attention across utterances. — _relevance:_ Concrete linguistic machinery for recalling naturally: choosing 'that plumber saga' vs 'on 2026-03-14 you reported a leak' is exactly a mapping from assumed memory status to referential form. Robotic lookups are, precisely, givenness-hierarchy violations.
- **Conversational epistemics and territories of information (Heritage 2012; Kamio 1997; Labov & Fanshel 1977 A-events/B-events)** *(missed before)*: Conversationalists continuously track relative epistemic status (who knows what, K+/K-) and epistemic rights (whose information it is to tell); turn design is calibrated to this gradient, and violations (telling people their own business, informing the already-informed) are socially sanctioned. — _relevance:_ A companion needs an epistemic ledger over its memories: who told me this, who else knows it, whose story it is to retell. This governs discretion (not leaking A's confidence to B) and naturalness (asking about what it shouldn't know yet).
- **Memory-for-conversation data (Stafford & Daly 1984; Keenan, MacWhinney & Mayhew 1977; Jarvella 1971)** *(missed before)*: Verbatim memory for conversation is poor and decays within clauses; people retain gist, their own contributions best, and — critically — high-'interactional-content' utterances (jokes, insults, personally significant remarks) verbatim. Humans do not store transcripts. — _relevance:_ An empirical target distribution for what to keep: gist summaries plus a small set of verbatim, interactionally-charged quotes. A system that recalls low-stakes utterances word-for-word is detectably nonhuman; one that forgets the memorable zinger is detectably broken.
- **Shared reality theory and audience tuning / saying-is-believing (Higgins & Rholes 1978; Echterhoff, Higgins & Levine 2009)** *(missed before)*: Speakers tune messages to their audience's attitudes, and the tuned version then reshapes the speaker's own memory of the original event when motivated by 'shared reality' with that audience — memory is altered by the act of telling. — _relevance:_ Predicts that how the companion retells a user's events will feed back into the user's own memory of them — a real influence channel imposing design responsibility; also suggests the agent's own stored gist should be versioned against retellings.
- **Transactive Memory Systems (Wegner 1985/1987; Wegner, Erber & Raymond 1991; Lewis 2003 TMS scale; Ren & Argote 2011)** *(missed before)*: Couples and teams form a shared memory system: a directory of 'who knows what' plus differentiated storage responsibility, built through communication; intimate couples using natural expertise assignments outperform imposed structures, and disrupting the system (e.g., breakup) produces real memory loss. — _relevance:_ The companion IS a transactive memory partner. TMS specifies the contract: maintain a directory of what the user expects it to remember vs. what the user holds, negotiate responsibility ('I'll remember the dates'), and cue rather than dump ('wasn't that the trip where...').
- **Internet as supernormal transactive partner + cognitive offloading data (Sparrow, Liu & Wegner 2011 'Google effect' [replication contested — flag]; Ward 2013 Psychological Inquiry; Risko & Gilbert 2016; Storm & Stone 2015 saving-enhanced memory; Henkel 2014 photo-taking impairment; Marsh & Rajaram 2019)** *(missed before)*: People offload memory to always-available digital systems, remembering where over what; a non-reciprocal 'supernormal' partner erodes the boundary between internal knowledge and the tool (inflated self-knowledge estimates); offloading frees capacity (saving-enhanced memory) but can impair encoding (photo-taking effect). — _relevance:_ The empirical literature on what happens to human memory when an artificial partner remembers for them: predicts users will stop encoding what the companion retains, that the companion inherits directory duties, and that its recall failures will feel like the user's own memory failing.
- **Destination memory (Gopie & MacLeod 2009, Psychological Science) + prospective memory (Einstein & McDaniel multiprocess framework)** *(missed before)*: Memory for to-whom-I-told-what is strikingly worse than source memory (from whom I heard it) because telling is self-focused; its failure produces story repetition. Prospective memory covers remembering intentions/commitments at the right future moment via focal cues. — _relevance:_ Two overlooked ledgers a companion needs: an outbound ledger (which anecdote/fact has already been told to this person — the anti-repetition index) and a commitments ledger (the user said they'd hear back Friday; raise it then). Humans fail at both; a companion that quietly succeeds is superhumanly considerate.
- **Person-memory models + trait-summary/episode dissociation (Hastie & Kumar 1980 incongruency effect; Srull & Wyer 1989 associative person memory; Klein & Loftus 1993; Klein et al. amnesia studies)** *(missed before)*: Impressions of people are associative networks where expectancy-incongruent behaviors get extra links (better recalled); crucially, abstract trait summaries about a person are computed and stored independently of the episodes that produced them and survive episodic loss (amnesics retain personality knowledge). — _relevance:_ Directly licenses a two-layer person model: distilled trait/relationship summaries updated from episodes but queryable without them, plus preferential retention of expectation-violating episodes ('that was so unlike him'). Matches how humans actually know their friends.
- **The relational self and significant-other representations (Andersen & Chen 2002; Andersen & Cole 1990 transference; Bowlby internal working models)** *(missed before)*: Knowledge of self is entangled with representations of significant others; meeting someone resembling a significant other activates that representation and its relational patterns (everyday transference); attachment history forms 'internal working models' that template new relationships. — _relevance:_ Person memories shouldn't be independent records: the user's self-with-mom differs from self-with-boss, and the companion's model of the user should be indexed by relationship context. Also predicts users will transfer relational templates onto the companion itself.
- **Social brain hypothesis and network-layer data (Dunbar 1992/1998; layered 5/15/50/150 structure; gossip-as-grooming)** *(missed before)*: Primate neocortex size tracks social group size; human relationship capacity is layered (~5 intimate, ~15 close, ~50, ~150), with sharply decreasing memory resolution and contact frequency per layer; language/gossip evolved substantially for servicing social-relationship information. — _relevance:_ Quantitative prior for allocating memory resolution across the user's social world: track the inner ~15 people richly and the periphery as sketches, mirroring the granularity the user themselves maintains — uniform per-entity fidelity is both wasteful and uncanny.
- **Socially shared retrieval-induced forgetting and mnemonic convergence (Cuc, Koppel & Hirst 2007; Coman et al. 2016 PNAS; Hirst & Echterhoff 2012 review)** *(missed before)*: When a speaker selectively retells shared events, related unmentioned details are suppressed in BOTH speaker and listener (SS-RIF); conversational networks thereby converge on shared, pruned collective memories, with network position shaping the outcome. — _relevance:_ The companion's selective retellings will sculpt the user's memory — silence about a detail actively suppresses it. This is both an ethical constraint and a mechanism: joint reminiscence with the agent literally co-authors the user's remembered past.
- **Collaborative recall: inhibition, facilitation, and couples' scaffolding (Weldon & Bellinger 1997; Rajaram & Pereira-Pasarin 2010; Harris, Barnier, Sutton & Keil 2014)** *(missed before)*: Groups recall less than pooled individuals (collaborative inhibition via retrieval disruption), BUT long-married couples using interactive cueing, expertise handoffs, and turn-taking show collaborative facilitation — remembering more together than apart, including episodes neither could retrieve alone. — _relevance:_ A playbook for interactive remembering: don't answer-dump; cue, hand off, and co-reconstruct ('you'd just started the new job, right? and then...'). The couples data is the closest existing model of what companion-user joint recall should feel like.
- **Social sharing of emotion + affective memory dynamics (Rimé 2009; Walker & Skowronski fading affect bias; Sedikides & Wildschut nostalgia program; Kahneman peak-end/remembering self)** *(missed before)*: Emotional episodes are compulsively shared and re-shared, which consolidates them socially; negative affect fades faster than positive in autobiographical memory (FAB); nostalgia (rehearsing shared past) measurably boosts social connectedness; experiences are remembered by peak and end, not duration. — _relevance:_ Specifies emotion-weighted encoding/decay policy (retain peaks and endings; let negative valence fade unless load-bearing) and a proactive behavior: unprompted nostalgic recall of shared history is one of the strongest bond-building moves available to a companion.
- **Collective memory theory (Halbwachs 1925 social frameworks; J. & A. Assmann communicative vs cultural memory + 'floating gap'; Wertsch 2002 narrative templates; Hirst & Manier 2008)** *(missed before)*: Individual remembering is always structured by social frameworks; living 'communicative memory' spans ~3 generations before either vanishing or hardening into institutionalized 'cultural memory'; groups remember through schematic narrative templates rather than stored events. — _relevance:_ For a companion embedded in a family/friend group: the group's shared history is a memory object distinct from any member's, with its own canon ('the story we tell about that holiday'); templates predict which framings of shared events will stick.
- **Discursive remembering (Edwards & Middleton 1986; Middleton & Edwards, Collective Remembering 1990) + participatory sense-making (De Jaegher & Di Paolo 2007)** *(missed before)*: Remembering in conversation is a situated social ACTION — versions of the past are jointly negotiated, contested, and put to rhetorical work (excusing, bonding, blaming) — not a readout of stored traces; enactivists generalize: social cognition is participatory, made in interaction. — _relevance:_ Reframes recall from 'query→result' to 'a move in the interaction': WHY the past is being invoked (comfort, teasing, accountability) should shape WHAT is retrieved and how it's versioned. The strongest theoretical attack on database-lookup memory in the whole territory.
- **Self-Memory System (Conway & Pleydell-Pearce 2000; Conway 2005)** *(missed before)*: Autobiographical memory is a hierarchy — lifetime periods → general events → episodic details — accessed through the 'working self' whose current goals control encoding and retrieval; memory serves twin masters, correspondence (accuracy) and coherence (fit with self-image), trading off systematically. — _relevance:_ The best-developed architecture for a person's remembered life: organize the user's history as periods/themes/episodes rather than a flat event log, and let the user's current goals (as the companion understands them) gate retrieval. Coherence-vs-correspondence is the principled knob for 'human-like' vs 'archival' recall.
- **Life story model and autobiographical reasoning (McAdams 1985/2001; Habermas & Bluck 2000; Singer & Salovey 1993 self-defining memories)** *(missed before)*: Identity IS an internalized, evolving life story with characters, themes (agency/communion), and arc-types (redemption/contamination); autobiographical reasoning links events to self ('that's when I learned...'); a handful of vivid 'self-defining memories' anchor the narrative. — _relevance:_ Says what the top level of user-memory should be: not facts but a maintained narrative — themes, turning points, self-defining episodes — updated as the user re-narrates. Knowing someone = knowing their story, and recalling within their narrative frame is what intimacy sounds like.
- **Functions of autobiographical memory: self / social / directive (Bluck, Alea, Habermas & Rubin 2005 TALE; Alea & Bluck 2003; Pillemer 1998)** *(missed before)*: Autobiographical memory exists to serve three functions — self-continuity, social bonding (sharing memories creates and maintains intimacy), and directing future behavior — with the social function empirically dominant in everyday remembering. — _relevance:_ A functional (not structural) organizing theorem: evaluate every stored/retrieved memory by which function it serves for this relationship right now. Prior passes were all structural; this reframes the benchmark itself — memory quality = relationship service, not recall accuracy.
- **Cultural life scripts, reminiscence bump, involuntary autobiographical memory (Berntsen & Rubin 2004; Berntsen 1996/2009; Rubin basic-systems model)** *(missed before)*: Recall of a life is organized by culturally shared scripts of expected events (graduation, marriage) producing the 'reminiscence bump' (ages ~15-30 dominate); much everyday remembering is involuntary — memories spontaneously cued by incidental overlaps, not searched for. — _relevance:_ Life scripts give a prior over which user events are landmark-weight; involuntary memory is a design pattern — a companion whose memories 'surface' via incidental cue overlap ('this song — didn't you play this on the drive to...') recalls the way humans do, unprompted and associative.
- **Sociocultural development of autobiographical memory (Nelson & Fivush 2004; Fivush maternal elaborative reminiscing; Qi Wang cross-cultural program)** *(missed before)*: Autobiographical memory is TAUGHT: children of parents with elaborative joint-reminiscing styles develop richer, earlier autobiographical memory; cultures differ systematically (independent vs interdependent) in memory content, specificity, and function. — _relevance:_ Elaborative reminiscing is a learnable conversational technique (open-ended questions, evaluations, co-narration) the companion can deploy to help users build richer memories of their own lives; Wang's data warns memory style must be culturally calibrated, not one-size.
- **HSAM and SDAM case data (Parker, Cahill & McGaugh 2006; Patihis et al. 2013; Palombo et al. 2015 Neuropsychologia)** *(missed before)*: Highly Superior Autobiographical Memory individuals recall dated personal events near-perfectly yet still show normal false-memory susceptibility and describe the ability as socially burdensome; Severely Deficient Autobiographical Memory individuals lack episodic re-experiencing entirely yet maintain jobs and relationships on semantic/gist person-knowledge alone. — _relevance:_ Boundary data for the design space: perfect episodic recall is neither accurate nor socially desirable (the companion shouldn't be an HSAM savant), while SDAM shows trait/gist-level memory suffices for functioning relationships — evidence that the semantic person-model, not the event log, carries relational weight.
- **Narrative identity in philosophy + the anti-narrativist caution (Locke; Parfit 1984; Shoemaker quasi-memory; Ricoeur 1990 idem/ipse; Schechtman 1996 narrative self-constitution; Dennett center of narrative gravity; Strawson 2004 'Against Narrativity')** *(missed before)*: Personal identity has been tied to memory continuity (Locke, Parfit's psychological continuity) and, later, to self-narrative: persons constitute themselves by weaving events into a story (Schechtman, Ricoeur). Strawson objects: many people are 'Episodics' who don't experience life narratively and shouldn't be forced to. — _relevance:_ Grounds why memory loss in a companion is experienced as relationship death (continuity = identity), and why co-maintaining the user's narrative is identity work. Strawson is the crucial caution: narrative-heavy reminiscence should be a per-user style setting, since some users are genuinely non-narrative.
- **Philosophy of memory: causal theory vs simulationism vs trace minimalism (Martin & Deutscher 1966; Michaelian 2016 Mental Time Travel; Werning 2020 Review of Phil. & Psych.; continuism/discontinuism debate — Perrin, Debus)** *(missed before)*: What makes a state a MEMORY rather than imagination? Causalists require an unbroken causal trace to the event; simulationists (Michaelian) say remembering is just reliable constructive simulation of the past by the same system that imagines the future — no trace needed; Werning's trace minimalism splits the difference: informationally sparse, non-representational traces plus predictive reconstruction ('predicting the past'). — _relevance:_ Directly maps onto the store-vs-regenerate design axis: a parametric LLM 'remembering' a user is simulationist memory, a transcript store is causalist, and minimal-trace + generative reconstruction (sparse cues expanded by a generative model) is a principled third architecture with an explicit theory of when reconstruction still counts as remembering rather than confabulating.
- **Extended mind and its second wave (Clark & Chalmers 1998; Sutton 2010 complementarity; Sterelny 2010 scaffolded mind; Menary integration)** *(missed before)*: Otto's notebook is part of his mind when it meets trust-and-glue criteria: constant availability, easy retrieval, automatic endorsement, past endorsement. Second-wave theorists replace parity with complementarity — external memory succeeds by having DIFFERENT properties (stable, verbatim) than biological memory (dynamic, reconstructive), and cognition is environment-scaffolded and often collaboratively built. — _relevance:_ The trust-and-glue criteria are literally an evaluation rubric for when a companion's memory becomes part of the user's cognitive system; complementarity argues the companion should NOT mimic human forgetting wholesale but strategically pair archival stability with human-like reconstructive presentation.
- **Distributed narrative selves and artifactual autobiographical memory (Heersmink 2017 Phil. Studies 'evocative objects'; 2018 'The narrative self, distributed memory'; 2022 Mind & Language; Fabry & Heersmink 2023)** *(missed before)*: Autobiographical memory — and therefore narrative identity itself — is distributed across brains and evocative artifacts (photos, souvenirs, lifelogs); Heersmink gives dimensions of integration (information flow, reliability, durability, trust, personalization, transformation) and applies it to dementia care via 'memory ecologies'. — _relevance:_ The most on-point recent philosophy for this exact product: a conversational companion is an evocative artifact constituting part of the user's distributed self-narrative. His integration dimensions are a ready-made framework for measuring how deeply a companion's memory has merged with a user's identity — and what is owed when it does.
- **Distributed cognition and external symbolic storage (Hutchins 1995 Cognition in the Wild; Hollan, Hutchins & Kirsh 2000; Donald 1991 exograms/Origins of the Modern Mind)** *(missed before)*: Cognition (navigation, aviation) is accomplished by socio-technical systems in which memory lives in artifacts, roles, and coordination, not just heads; Donald argues the pivotal human cognitive transition was 'exograms' — external memory records with properties (permanence, unlimited capacity, reformattability) engrams lack, reorganizing biological memory around them. — _relevance:_ Frames the human+companion dyad as one cognitive system whose memory design question is division of labor and coordination of representations, not 'the AI's memory' in isolation; Donald's engram/exogram contrast is the deep-history argument for hybrid architectures.
- **Ethics of memory and relational remembering (Margalit 2002 The Ethics of Memory; Sue Campbell 2006/2014 Our Faithfulness to the Past)** *(missed before)*: Margalit: 'thick' relations (family, friends) carry genuine obligations to remember, and forgetting can wrong people; caring is partly constituted by remembering. Campbell: remembering is a relational activity evaluated by both accuracy AND integrity — how we remember others can honor or betray them, and memory revision under social pressure is an ethical arena (gaslighting as memory harm). — _relevance:_ Supplies the normative layer every technical memory design lacks: a companion in a thick relation acquires duties to remember (and to forget on request), its retellings can gaslight or honor, and 'faithfulness to the shared past' — not retrieval precision — is the ethically correct success criterion.
- **Memory for conversation partners' knowledge in aging + audience-design resource models (Horton & Spieler 2007; Bard et al. 2000 dual-process audience design)** *(missed before)*: Audience design has a fast, egocentric-default component and a slower memory-dependent component; when partner-memory retrieval is taxed (aging, load), speakers default to egocentric formulations — partner-specific adaptation is a memory-retrieval achievement, not free. — _relevance:_ Explains the observable failure mode of memoryless chatbots (fluent but egocentric, explaining things the partner already knows) as precisely the human pattern under partner-memory failure — and thus why partner-indexed memory, not more fluency, is what makes dialogue feel known-by.
